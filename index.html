Abortion as a Non-Moral Standard

It's not solely that fetuses are alive biologically. They are also not simply biologically human, unlike skin cells or organs. In terms of biology, they are human beings. Some thinkers contend that because we are human beings who are physically connected to fetuses who were human beings, abortion is wrong. They seem to contend that because we have qualities that make it wrong to kill us now (prima facie wrong to kill: wrong unless extraordinary circumstances justify the killing), it was also wrong to kill us at any point in our development because we have always been the same organism, the same being. This is due to the fact that their nerve systems and brains aren't sufficiently mature and intricately integrated to support consciousness and personality. This developmental stage is not reached until after the first trimester, or more likely, until mid-pregnancy, according to medical and scientific research.5 The majority of abortions take place very early in pregnancy, killing fetuses who are not yet conscious and are therefore not yet persons according to this theory of personhood. However, unless carried out for a medically justifiable reason, any subsequent abortions affecting conscious and feeling fetuses who are individuals or close to it would likely be incorrect according to this theory. However, just because something (or someone) is not a person does not necessarily imply that killing it is not wicked. Even if fetuses are not yet people, they may one day become people. (And hypothetical people are never real people.) Does such potential make it morally wrong to kill embryos, for example, or grant them a right to life in any other way? Potential adults, spouses, criminals, doctors, and judges would all have the same rights as actual ones if potential things had the same rights as actual things. It is conceivable that potential personhood does not confer the same rights as real personhood because they don't. Since potentiality never does that for anything else, at the very least, we are entitled to an explanation of why it would. Even if a fetus is not yet a person, abortion may seem to prohibit it from enjoying its valued future, just as murdering us does.6 However, our futures may be valuable in part because we can currently look forward to them. One significant distinction between the futures of fetuses and our futures is that fetuses are completely unaware of their destinies.
